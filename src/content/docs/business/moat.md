Rambo is **deliberately anti-lock-in** (local files, open format, no hostage data). So the moat can't be "you can't leave." It comes from six places:

## 1. Proprietary depth
The **LLM-testing engine** and the **agent integration** are the hardest-to-copy assets — closed-source, and a genuine engineering investment.

## 2. Verifiable-local-first brand
In a market defined by *betrayed* trust, "the client that provably never holds your data hostage" is a durable position — the Obsidian reputation model.

## 3. Open format as anti-lock-in proof
"Closed app, open format" substitutes for open source: users can always walk away with their data, which paradoxically makes them comfortable staying.

## 4. Agent-ecosystem gravity
If AI agents default to Rambo's MCP server and CLI, we own the workflow — a compounding position as agent-driven development grows.

## 5. Team network effects + enterprise switching costs
Shared workspaces create team gravity; enterprise governance integration (SSO/SCIM/RBAC/audit) creates real switching cost at the top of the funnel.

## 6. Self-hosting as a compliance advantage — *not a giveaway*
When a customer self-hosts, **they** become data controller *and* processor, and **we have no access to their end-user data** — removing our GDPR obligations and shrinking our compliance surface (the PostHog pattern, verified). So the enterprise tier simultaneously **serves** regulated buyers *and* **reduces our own** burden. And — the Plausible lesson — self-hosting is a **trust/adoption channel**, monetized via license + support, **not** a donation-funded revenue line; at-scale management features stay paid.

## Honest caveats
- **Yaak overlaps ~70%** of the positioning → defend on git-native + LLM-testing depth + the full agent suite.
- **Streaming-render basics are catchable** → defend with the full agentic-debug + cost/eval + MCP suite, not the easy part.
- **Closed-source** invites enterprise SBOM/source-access asks → plan **source escrow**.
